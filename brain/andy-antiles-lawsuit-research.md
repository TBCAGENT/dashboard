## Causes of Action (8 Claims)

### 1. Breach of Contract (Written - LOI)
**Strength: STRONG**
- LOI signed by both parties
- Clear ownership terms: "Luke Fontaine would be awarded one-third (1/3) ownership"
- Exit Clause requires proportional buyout
- Defendants breached by: (a) refusing to acknowledge ownership, (b) refusing buyout, (c) withholding distributions

**Defense risk:** LOI says "not a legally binding document"
**Counter:** Parties' conduct treated it as binding (distributions, bank filings, internal records)

### 2. Breach of Oral Contract
**Strength: STRONG**
- September 29, 2023 agreement: 1/3 ownership for full-time work
- Confirmed in writing 2 days later via text
- Fully performed by Luke for 14 months

### 3. Breach of Implied Duty of Good Faith and Fair Dealing
**Strength: STRONG**
- Andy frustrated Luke's contractual benefits through deception
- Cut off access to financial records
- Refused to honor exit provisions

### 4. Promissory Estoppel
**Strength: VERY STRONG**
- Clear promises made (ownership, profit share)
- Luke reasonably relied (worked 14 months full-time)
- Detrimental reliance (foregone other opportunities)
- Injustice can only be avoided by enforcement
- **Key:** Works even if contract deemed unenforceable

### 5. Fraudulent Misrepresentation
**Strength: STRONG (if intent proven)**
- Andy made ownership representations to: Luke, Bank of America, in corporate records
- If Andy never intended to honor the deal, that's fraud
- **Key evidence:** The systematic documentation (bank filings, balance sheets, minutes) followed by sudden denial suggests premeditation
- **Punitive damages available**

### 6. Accounting
**Strength: PROCEDURAL - NECESSARY**
- Luke was cut off from financial records
- Cannot calculate exact damages without Graystone financials
- Court should order full disclosure

### 7. Breach of Fiduciary Duty
**Strength: STRONG**
- As LLC members, Andy owed Luke duty of care and loyalty
- Self-interested transactions (keeping Luke's share)
- Misrepresentations about ownership
- Improper withholding of distributions

### 8. Declaratory Relief
**Strength: PROCEDURAL**
- Seeks judicial determination of:
  - Luke's 1/3 ownership
  - Right to distributions
  - Enforceability of contracts
  - Obligation to buy out Luke's equity

## Potential Defense Arguments & Rebuttals

### Defense 1: "LOI is not legally binding"
The LOI explicitly states: "it is not a legally binding document"

**Rebuttal:**
- Parties' conduct overrides boilerplate language (California law)
- Actions demonstrated intent to be bound:
  - Monthly distributions made per LOI terms
  - Bank filings reflected ownership split
  - Internal financials labeled Luke as 33.33% owner
  - Andy introduced Luke as "my business partner"
- Course of dealing for 14 months creates implied contract

### Defense 2: "No formal operating agreement"
The LOI says parties "anticipate further discussions to draft a formal partnership agreement"

**Rebuttal:**
- Oral contracts are enforceable in California
- Text message and LOI memorialize essential terms
- Performance (14 months of work + distributions) validates agreement
- Promissory estoppel applies regardless of formality

### Defense 3: "Luke was just an employee with profit-sharing"
Andy might argue Luke was an employee, not owner

**Rebuttal:**
- Bank filing specifically declared Luke as "beneficial owner" (not employee)
- LOI uses "ownership" language, not employment
- Luke had signing authority on bank accounts
- Luke had decision-making authority over hiring, operations, partnerships
- Title: VP and COO (officer, not just employee)

### Defense 4: "Valuation is inflated"
Andy might dispute the $4M valuation

**Rebuttal:**
- Discovery will reveal actual financials
- Accounting cause of action addresses this
- "Seven figure monthly revenue" × standard multiples supports valuation
- Company has 3,000+ students at $7K each = substantial recurring revenue
